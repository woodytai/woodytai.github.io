Grief behind a smile

Grief behind a smile

Boys don't cry It might be sexist but growing up in Hong Kong meant that I was in a society where stoicism is part of the culture. My parents and so many before them were always told that showing vulnerability was a weakness. My parents tried to distance me from that mentality, but with relatives and friends, it became hard.

I was complimented by my teachers (at least according to my mom) because of how I'd always smile, which growing up, slowly developed into this image that no matter the challenge I faced, whenever there were difficulties whether from academic strides to adolescence dreams â€” I needed to smile through it all.

This slowly morphed into If I smiled, no one would pry or ask! because deep down I knew that if someone looked me in the eye and asked Are you ok? I'd break down on the spot. This facade became my shield, protecting me from the discomfort of other's potential reactions.


I worried that if I revealed even an ounce of sadness, I might lose all the potential in other's eyes that I so dearly want. After all who wants to be seen as needy and moody, right?

As I navigated my confused teenage years, this seemed to finally set in and almost forced me to conform to this society's 'norm' to fit in. The more I looked at my peers who seemed to handle hurdles easily, the more I buried my emotions.

Now onto a bit of a psych lesson, you can't choose to only feel some emotions, it was mostly black and white, meaning, You can't just bury sadness, you have to bury every emotion on the face of this earth, which thinking back was really toxic.

Yet I still buried my emotions like it was every Tuesday, the more they built up. Like a dam with the water level rising by the day, eventually leading to the uncontrolled release of impounded emotions, rushing over my body like ants crawling all over, everything seemed to spin and my heart went into overdrive. As every single emotion I've repressed floods out, a melting pot of insecurities and emotions washes over me, but not as gentle waves but rather as a tsunami with hundreds of feet worth of water overwhelming every sense of my body, my palace I so dearly fought for, where every single insecurity is amplified and examined.

â–¡

After 2 years of living as if I was on a roller-coaster, thinking Thank god, they didn't realise how stressed I was or Phew, thank god they didn't ask about me! and the next moment judging myself as if I was a saint and demanded perfection, looking over every flaw and mistake I have.

Now I take a different approach, I still have the dam sitting there, just not releasing it when it inevitably breaks, but rather when I have a moment to wind down and take a peek into who I really am.
